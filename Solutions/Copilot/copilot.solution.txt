## 1. How much unclaimed money is lying in the Indian financial system?

These numbers move every year, but directionally they’re huge. I’ll give you **approximate, order‑of‑magnitude figures**, not fantasy precision.

### Unclaimed bank deposits
- **Public and private banks** transfer “unclaimed deposits” (no customer activity for 10+ years) to a special RBI‑managed account.
- Recent public statements and reports indicate:
  - Earlier figures (around 2022–23) were in the range of **₹40,000–₹50,000 crore**.
  - More recent updates suggest this has grown further, crossing **₹60,000 crore+**.
- PSU banks hold the majority; SBI alone typically accounts for a large share.

### Unclaimed EPF / PF
- EPFO has a large pool of **inoperative / dormant accounts** where members or nominees never claimed.
- Older official and RTI‑based analyses have put this in the **tens of thousands of crores** (e.g., **₹20,000–₹30,000 crore+** range).
- A portion of this is gradually being claimed, but new inoperative balances keep adding.

### Unclaimed NPS / pension‑type funds
- NPS is relatively newer and more structured; unclaimed amounts are **much smaller** compared to EPF and bank deposits.
- However, there are still:
  - Accounts where subscribers die and nominees never claim.
  - Small balances that remain idle.

### Unclaimed insurance (life & general)
- Life insurers and general insurers maintain “unclaimed amounts” (matured policies, unpaid claims, etc.).
- Industry‑wide, this runs into **several thousand crores**.
- A portion of this is periodically transferred to the **Senior Citizens’ Welfare Fund** if unclaimed for a long period.

### Unclaimed mutual funds, shares, dividends
- Unclaimed dividends, matured deposits, and shares are transferred to the **Investor Education and Protection Fund (IEPF)**.
- The IEPF corpus is in the **thousands of crores** and growing.
- Additionally, mutual funds maintain unclaimed redemption/dividend amounts.

### Big picture
If you aggregate across:
- Banks  
- EPF/other PF  
- Insurance  
- Mutual funds, shares, dividends  

You’re easily looking at **₹1–2 lakh crore** of unclaimed financial assets in India, and growing.

The **root cause** is exactly what you described:  
people die, information is scattered, and families don’t even know what to claim.

## 3. Common problems families face after death of the main person

Here we’re talking about **overall problems**, not just money.

### A. Information invisibility
- Family doesn’t know:
  - Which banks he used  
  - Where FDs are  
  - Which LIC / private policies exist  
  - Where demat / mutual funds are

### B. Documentation and access issues
- Physical documents:
  - Locked in cupboards, lockers, offices, or other cities.
  - Misplaced, damaged, or never printed (e‑statements only).
- Digital access:
  - No passwords for email, net banking, demat, trading apps.
  - No access to registered mobile number (SIM deactivated, or in the deceased’s name).

### C. Nominee vs legal heir confusion
- Nominee not updated or missing.
- Nominee is different from legal heir → disputes.
- Nominee is a minor → guardian/legal process needed.
- Multiple nominees across different products, no consistency.

### D. Legal and procedural complexity
- Need for:
  - Death certificate  
  - Legal heir certificate / succession certificate  
  - Probate of will (if any)  
- Each institution (bank, insurer, EPFO, RTA, etc.) has its own forms and process.
- Long timelines, repeated visits, and follow‑ups.

### E. Emotional overload
- Family is grieving and mentally exhausted.
- They postpone paperwork → limitation periods, more complications.
- Often they **give up on smaller amounts**, which then become “unclaimed”.

## 4. Financial problems after death of the main breadwinner

Now, specifically **financial pain**:

### A. Immediate liquidity crunch
- Bank accounts may be frozen or inaccessible until formalities are done.
- Salary stops immediately.
- EMIs, rent, school fees, medical bills, household expenses continue.
- Insurance claims (if any) take time to process.

### B. No clear picture of total assets and liabilities
- Family doesn’t know:
  - How much money is actually available.
  - What loans exist (personal loans, credit cards, business loans).
  - Which EMIs are auto‑debited from which accounts.
- Risk of:
  - Missing EMIs → penalties, credit score damage, legal notices.
  - Overlooking assets → money left unclaimed.

### C. Low financial literacy among dependents
- Spouse or parents may not understand:
  - How to file claims.
  - How to deal with banks, insurers, EPFO, etc.
  - How to invest or safely park the money they receive.
- High risk of:
  - Mis-selling by agents.
  - Fraud by relatives or intermediaries.
  - Rapid depletion of funds.

### D. Long-term insecurity
- If the deceased was the **sole or primary earner**, the family may:
  - Drop in lifestyle.
  - Struggle with children’s education, healthcare, retirement.
  - Be forced to sell property or gold at distress prices.

- Margins are low unless you automate discovery and documentation.

- **Digital layer** → asset registry, document vault, death-trigger release, guided workflows.

## What the product must do (core architecture)
A successful product in this space needs three pillars.

### 1. Asset & document vault (pre-death)
A secure, private vault where a user can store:

- Bank accounts, FDs, RDs  
- EPF, NPS, PPF  
- Insurance policies  
- Mutual funds, demat, shares  
- Property documents  
- Gold, silver, lockers  
- Loans, liabilities  
- Passwords, digital accounts

The vault must support:

- **Conditional release** (“only after my death”)  
- **Trusted contacts** (spouse, children, executor)  
- **Periodic reminders** to update information

### 2. Death-triggered workflow engine
When the user dies, the system should:

- Notify trusted contacts  
- Release the vault  
- Generate a **personalized claim checklist**  
- Provide step-by-step instructions for each asset type  
- Auto-fill forms where possible  
- Track progress across institutions  

This is the “digital brain” of the product.

### 3. Human assistance layer (post-death)
A claims concierge team that:

- Helps families collect documents  
- Files claims with banks, insurers, EPFO, RTAs  
- Coordinates with government offices  
- Supports legal processes (succession certificate, probate, mutation)  
- Provides emotional and administrative support  

This is where you build trust and revenue.
