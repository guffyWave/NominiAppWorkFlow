The problem is acute in public sector institutions (80% of bank unclaimed) and among low-literacy households.

### 3. What are all the common problems family faces post death of the main person?

Post-death challenges for Indian families are multifaceted, blending emotional, social, and financial strains. Based on research and case studies, common issues include:

- **Social Ostracism and Isolation**: Widows often face exclusion from family events, restricted mobility, and cultural stigmas (e.g., "inauspicious" status in some communities), leading to mental health issues like depression.
- **Abuse and Exploitation**: Verbal/physical abuse from in-laws, pressure to relinquish assets, or forced remarriage (levirate). Elderly dependents may be neglected.
- **Health and Well-being Deterioration**: Chronic issues like joint pain, respiratory problems, or mobility limitations worsen due to lack of care; men struggle with household chores post-spouse death.
- **Childcare and Education Disruptions**: Immature children face emotional trauma, school dropouts, or child labor if the deceased was the caregiver.
- **Legal and Inheritance Disputes**: Delays in proving heirship (e.g., obtaining succession certificates), especially without a will, leading to family conflicts or court battles (2-5 years average).
- **Access to Basic Needs**: Homelessness if disowned, reliance on charity, or turning to informal work/begging/prostitution in extreme cases.
- **Entire Family Loss Scenarios**: In accidents (e.g., car crashes), distant relatives struggle with asset identification and claims, exacerbating unclaimed funds.

These are prevalent in rural/low-income families but affect urban ones too, per UN and Indian studies  .

### 4. What are the problems people face with finances post-death of the main bread earner of the family.

Financial fallout from losing the breadwinner is acute in India, where 70-80% of households rely on a single earner. Key problems:

- **Immediate Income Loss**: Sudden halt in salary/pension/business income, leading to inability to pay bills, EMIs, or daily expenses. Families often borrow at high rates or sell assets (e.g., gold).
- **Medical Debt Burden**: Pre-death treatment costs (e.g., 10-20% of unclaimed arise from illness-related debts) push families into poverty; widows repay from meager sources.
- **Asset Access Barriers**: Scattered accounts (banks, EPF, NPS) unknown to nominees; delays in claims (4-6 months average) due to paperwork mismatches or unawareness .
- **Unclaimed Funds Accumulation**: Families unaware of policies/assets; e.g., only 4/9 surveyed knew claim processes, learned post-loss.
- **Tax and Legal Complications**: Filing deceased's ITR, transferring assets without will (succession certificate costs ₹5,000-50,000+), or NRI complications.
- **Long-Term Vulnerability**: Reduced savings for education/retirement; women enter low-wage informal jobs, perpetuating poverty cycles.

Research shows 15-20% annual growth in unclaimed due to these issues .

### 5. Who are the typical people who face this problem?

This issue disproportionately affects:

- **Widows and Single Mothers**: ~45 million widows in India (UN data); rural/low-income women with limited education face asset denial and poverty.
- **Families of Sole Breadwinners**: Salaried men/business owners (your problem's focus); middle-class urban families with scattered assets, or migrant workers' kin.
- **Elderly Dependents**: Retired parents reliant on deceased child; no pension access.
- **Immature Children/Orphans**: In full-family accidents, minors lack financial maturity; guardians struggle with claims.
- **Low-Literacy/Low-Income Households**: 70% of unclaimed from public banks; rural/semi-urban where sharing info is taboo.
- **NRIs or Migrants' Families**: Distant relatives unaware of assets abroad/India.

Demographics: 60% rural, women aged 40-60, lower-middle class (income ₹2-5 lakh/year).

### 6. What are the solutions people use in order to solve such situation. User segment?

Solutions vary by user segment, but focus on pre-death planning and post-death facilitation:

- **Wills and Nominations**: Register nominees for all accounts (banks, EPF, NPS, LIC); create a will (₹5,000-20,000 cost) for asset distribution. **User segment**: Educated middle-class (salaried/business, 30-50 years), urban professionals.
- **Life Insurance and Emergency Funds**: Term plans (₹10-20 lakh cover) for income replacement; build 6-12 months' expenses fund. **User segment**: Young families with dependents.
- **Digital Tools/Vaults**: Apps like DGLegacy or secure spreadsheets to store asset info, shared post-death. **User segment**: Tech-savvy millennials/gen-Z, NRIs.
- **Joint Accounts/Trusts**: Hold assets jointly; family trusts for privacy. **User segment**: High-net-worth business owners.
- **Financial Literacy/Advisors**: Use planners for inventory; government campaigns like "Your Money, Your Right". **User segment**: Low-income/rural via NGOs.
- **Post-Death Services**: Hire firms for claims (e.g., Yellow.in). **User segment**: All, but urban middle-class.

Adoption low (only 2/9 share details per surveys); solutions reduce unclaimed by 30-50% if implemented .
